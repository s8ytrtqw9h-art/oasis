INTRODUCTION

▼

Universities need strong reputations to attract students, staff, and partners. Employee advocacy, when staff promote the university's achievements and values, helps build trust and credibility (Clark, Chapleo, & Suomi, 2019; Santos et al., 2023).

Many African universities, including health sciences institutions, do not fully engage staff in advocacy due to limited communication or a lack of formal programs (Yaping et al., 2023).

This study explores how employees at Entrance University of Health Sciences act as brand ambassadors, what motivates or limits their advocacy, and how their actions impact the university's image.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

▼

Employee advocacy is becoming a vital part of university branding in today's competitive higher-education environment, as staff who share positive stories and values can significantly enhance institutional reputation (Clark, Chapleo, & Suomi, 2019; Santos et al., 2023).

At Entrance University of Health Sciences (EUHS), it is unclear how employees actually take part in advocacy, how they perceive their role as ambassadors, or how much their efforts influence the institution's visibility and brand strength. Research suggests that weak internal communication and limited support for internal branding frequently undermine staff engagement in brand-related behaviors (Ahmed, Shakur, Hashim, & Ahmed, 2024).

These gaps may prevent EUHS from fully leveraging its employees' potential as authentic brand advocates. Therefore, this study investigates the types of advocacy undertaken by EUHS staff, the motivations and barriers they face, and how their advocacy shapes the university's public image.

EXPANDED LITERATURE REVIEW

ENHANCED
▼

Employee Advocacy in Higher Education

Employee advocacy refers to the voluntary promotion of an organization by its staff through both formal and informal channels (Van Camp, 2023). In universities, this includes sharing achievements on social media, recommending the institution to prospective students, defending university policies, and embodying institutional values in daily interactions.

Value Alignment and Brand Equity

Employees are more likely to promote the university when their personal values match the institution's values (Clark, Chapleo, & Suomi, 2019). Value consonance strengthens connection to the brand and fosters employee-based brand equity (Yang et al., 2022). This alignment creates authentic advocacy that resonates more powerfully with external stakeholders than traditional marketing.

Internal Branding and Communication

Effective internal branding requires consistent communication, leadership support, and organizational culture that values employee contribution (Duh & Wara, 2023). African universities often face challenges in this area due to hierarchical structures and limited resources for internal communication programs.

Research Gaps

Most employee advocacy research focuses on Western contexts and digital advocacy. Limited studies examine non-digital advocacy or explore the phenomenon in African health sciences universities, where unique cultural and organizational factors may influence advocacy behaviors differently.

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

▼

Social Exchange Theory (Blau, 1964)

Employees advocate when they feel valued, supported, and fairly treated. The theory posits that individuals engage in behaviors based on a cost-benefit analysis of their relationships. When universities invest in employees through recognition, development, and supportive environments, employees reciprocate through advocacy behaviors.

Intrinsic Motivation Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985)

Emotional and internal drives encourage voluntary advocacy behaviors. This self-determination theory suggests that when employees experience autonomy, competence, and relatedness in their work environment, they are intrinsically motivated to promote their organization without external rewards.

Value Alignment Theory (Clark, Chapleo, & Suomi, 2019)

Advocacy becomes authentic when employees' personal values match institutional values. This congruence creates psychological ownership and emotional attachment to the brand, leading to more genuine and persuasive advocacy.

ENHANCED METHODOLOGY

ENHANCED
▼

Research Design

Qualitative case study design using interpretive phenomenological approach to explore employees' lived experiences and perceptions of brand advocacy at EUHS.

Research Population

All permanent employees of Entrance University of Health Sciences across academic, administrative, and support staff categories.

Sampling Method & Justification

Purposive Sampling will be used to select information-rich participants who can provide deep insights into employee advocacy practices.

Sample Size & Composition

15 participants selected to achieve theoretical saturation, distributed as follows:

  • 5 Academic staff (lecturers, professors across departments)
  • 5 Administrative staff (HR, communications, admissions)
  • 3 Support staff (IT, library, student services)
  • 2 Mid-level managers (heads of department, coordinators)

This size aligns with qualitative research conventions where 12-15 interviews typically achieve saturation (Guest et al., 2006).

Inclusion Criteria

  • Minimum 1 year employment at EUHS
  • Active engagement in university activities
  • Willingness to participate voluntarily
  • Diverse representation across departments and roles

Data Collection Instrument

Semi-structured interviews (45-60 minutes each) with open-ended questions covering:

  • Understanding of university brand and values
  • Personal advocacy behaviors and motivations
  • Perceived organizational support for advocacy
  • Barriers to advocacy participation
  • Impact perceptions of advocacy efforts

Data Collection Procedure

  1. Obtain ethical approval from university research committee
  2. Contact potential participants via email with information sheet
  3. Schedule interviews at participants' convenience
  4. Obtain written informed consent before each interview
  5. Audio-record interviews with permission
  6. Transcribe recordings verbatim within 48 hours

Data Analysis

Thematic analysis using NVivo software following Braun & Clarke's (2006) six-phase framework:

  1. Familiarization with data through repeated reading
  2. Generating initial codes
  3. Searching for themes
  4. Reviewing and refining themes
  5. Defining and naming themes
  6. Producing the final report

Trustworthiness & Rigor

  • Credibility: Member checking and peer debriefing
  • Transferability: Thick description of context and participants
  • Dependability: Audit trail of all research decisions
  • Confirmability: Reflexive journal and triangulation with literature

STUDY LIMITATIONS

ENHANCED
▼
  • Generalizability: As a single case study, findings may not be transferable to other universities with different contexts
  • Self-Report Bias: Participants may overstate their advocacy behaviors due to social desirability
  • Sample Size: While appropriate for qualitative research, the sample may not capture all perspectives within EUHS
  • Time Constraints: Cross-sectional design captures perceptions at one point in time, not longitudinal changes
  • Researcher Bias: As researchers affiliated with communication studies, our interpretations may be influenced by theoretical predispositions (addressed through reflexivity)
